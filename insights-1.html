The Twelve-Month Window: Why Pre-Transaction Planning Cannot Be Compressed

The most consequential decisions surrounding a sale are made in the year before signing — and most are foreclosed by the time a letter of intent arrives.

The pattern is familiar enough to be predictable. A founder spends fifteen years building an enterprise. A banker is engaged. Conversations with prospective acquirers begin in earnest. A letter of intent arrives, sometimes on a Friday. And in the weeks that follow, the founder's advisors are asked, in essence, to undo a decade of accumulated entity decisions in time for a closing now scheduled ninety days out.

It is rarely possible. Many of the most useful planning techniques available to a private business owner — basis adjustments, entity reorganizations, intra-family transfers, charitable structuring, residency planning, qualified small business stock analysis — share a common feature. They require time. Not weeks of time. Months, and frequently years.

Why the timeline is structural, not preferential

The constraints on pre-transaction planning are largely written into the Internal Revenue Code and into the case law that surrounds it. Several recur often enough to be worth naming.

Step transaction doctrine. When a series of transactions occurs in close proximity to a sale, courts and the Service have a long-standing willingness to collapse those steps into a single transaction for tax purposes. Distance in time is one of the principal factors that distinguishes a planning step from a transaction step. The longer a planning step stands on its own — operating, paying its own bills, generating its own returns — the more likely it is to be respected on its own terms.

Substance over form. The Service's willingness to look past the structural form of a transaction to its underlying economic substance increases dramatically when a transaction is undertaken in obvious anticipation of a specific sale. Planning steps that have a meaningful operating life, that survive without reference to the eventual transaction, and that the family would have undertaken even if the transaction did not occur, are far more durable.

Holding periods. Several of the most important provisions available to founders — including the qualified small business stock provisions, the long-term capital gain rate, certain installment sale treatments, and a number of estate-related elections — turn on holding periods measured in years. A holding period clock cannot be retroactively started.

Valuation. Intra-family transfers, charitable transfers, and entity restructurings all rely on defensible valuation. The valuation of a private interest is meaningfully easier to defend before the company is in the market than during a live sale process, when the market is, in the most literal sense, telling the appraiser the answer.

What the twelve-month window enables that the ninety-day window does not

A twelve-month window is not a magic number. It is, in our experience, the practical horizon within which most of the techniques worth considering can be evaluated, decided upon, structured, documented, and given enough operating life to stand on their own.

Within that window, a number of conversations become possible. The principal can address residency in a way the audit posture is likely to respect. Trusts can be funded in a way the valuation is likely to survive. Entity structures can be rationalized and given a year of independent operating history. Charitable considerations can be addressed without the appearance of having been timed to a closing. The family's broader balance sheet can be addressed in coordination with, rather than after, the transaction.

None of these conversations require a transaction in hand. They require only that one is plausible.

The least valuable hour of pre-transaction planning is the one that arrives the week after the letter of intent.

What the ninety-day window still permits

It is worth being honest about what remains possible inside the ninety-day window between a signed letter of intent and a closing, because that is the window most principals find themselves in. The list is shorter than founders generally hope and longer than their late-arriving advisors generally admit.

Closing-window planning is principally documentary. The transaction structure can be reviewed. Allocation of purchase price can be negotiated. The mechanics of installment treatment, if available, can be optimized. Personal estate documents can be brought current. Charitable considerations addressed in the months that follow closing remain available to address a portion of the federal liability.

What is no longer available, by and large, is everything that requires a holding period that has already elapsed, an entity that has already operated, a residency that has already been established, or a valuation that does not have a transaction sitting on top of it.

A modest suggestion

The single most useful conversation a founder can have, in our view, is the one that occurs eighteen to thirty-six months before any transaction has been seriously contemplated. The conversation does not need to lead to a decision. It needs only to identify which decisions are coming and which of them have time-bound preconditions. Most do.

The decisions a founder forecloses by waiting are not, in any single case, catastrophic. They simply accumulate, and the cumulative effect — measured against a transaction the founder has spent a working lifetime building toward — is rarely small.
