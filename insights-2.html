Reading the IRS Strategic Operating Plan: What Sophisticated Taxpayers Should Anticipate

Recent enforcement priorities make clear that complex private structures and high-income passthroughs will face sustained examination resources for the foreseeable future.

The Strategic Operating Plan published by the Service in recent years is, properly read, a forecast. It tells advisors and taxpayers, in unusually plain language, what the agency intends to do with the resources Congress has appropriated to it. For the population of taxpayers our firm advises — closely-held business owners, high-income passthroughs, families with substantial concentrated holdings, and the structures organized around them — the document repays careful reading.

Three themes emerge.

1. A sustained shift toward complex private filers

The plan and its implementing guidance describe a multi-year reallocation of examination capacity toward partnership and S-corporation filings, large complex partnerships in particular, and toward individual filers reporting passthrough income above defined thresholds. Examination capacity in these segments has been historically thin relative to filing population, and the agency has been transparent about the intention to address that gap.

This is a technical posture, not a political one. The same reallocation has been described by successive administrations and is reflected in agency hiring and training pipelines that are, by their nature, multi-year commitments. The practical implication for advisors is straightforward: the probability that a given complex passthrough return will be examined is meaningfully higher today than it was five years ago, and is likely to remain so.

2. Renewed attention to listed and reportable transactions

The Service has signaled, repeatedly and publicly, an intention to bring more transaction categories within the listed transaction and reportable transaction frameworks, and to enforce the existing categories more aggressively. The recent treatment of certain conservation easement structures, micro-captive insurance arrangements, and abusive monetized installment sale variants illustrates the trajectory.

For sophisticated taxpayers, this trajectory has practical consequences that extend beyond the specific transaction categories named. It creates a meaningful examination penalty for any structure whose features overlap with, or resemble, structures the agency has previously challenged — even where the structure at issue is not itself listed. The standard professional question — does this structure resemble something the agency has challenged in the past? — has accumulated weight.

3. A material advisor enforcement posture

The disclosure obligations that fall on material advisors under the Internal Revenue Code are not new. The enforcement posture around them is. Recent guidance and recent litigation have made clear that the agency will pursue advisors, and not solely taxpayers, where it concludes that disclosure obligations have been ignored. The penalties are meaningful, and they fall on professionals personally.

This has implications both for firms that promote sophisticated structures and for the referring advisors who do not promote them but who, by virtue of providing tax opinions, marketing materials, or implementation services, can find themselves within the statutory definition of a material advisor.

What the plan does not say

The Strategic Operating Plan is also, at points, telling for what it does not address.

It does not announce a policy intention to disfavor sophisticated planning generally. The agency continues to acknowledge — and the courts continue to enforce — that taxpayers are entitled to arrange their affairs to minimize their tax liability within the limits of the Code. The frame the plan adopts is narrower: an emphasis on examination, reporting, and disclosure within an existing legal framework, not an expansion of that framework.

Nor does the plan, in our reading, suggest a categorical hostility toward private trust planning, generational transfer planning, or domestic charitable structuring. These remain areas of active practice, well-supported by statute and case law. The agency's posture is procedural, not substantive.

Practical implications for the planning we do

Three practical conclusions follow.

The documentation standard has gone up. Planning steps that would have been entirely defensible five years ago, on the documentation that was customary five years ago, are no longer adequately documented for today's examination posture. Contemporaneous valuation, contemporaneous economic substance analysis, and contemporaneous business purpose documentation are no longer optional.

Advisor selection matters more. The agency's increased focus on the material advisor framework places more weight on the rigor and independence of the advisors that opine on a given structure. Opinions that were treated as routine — and that, in some cases, were routine — now carry consequences for the advisor as well as the taxpayer.

The cost of category overlap has gone up. Structures that share material features with structures the agency has previously challenged — even where the structure at issue is not itself listed — now carry examination cost that may not have been priced into the original planning analysis. Sophisticated advisors are increasingly running this question affirmatively.

The defensibility of a structure today is judged against the documentation standard of today, not the documentation standard of the year it was implemented.

A note on temperament

The shift in examination posture is real, and the consequences for advisors and taxpayers are real. Neither warrants alarm. The same tools available to sophisticated taxpayers a decade ago remain available today; the standard for using them has simply tightened.

The firms that will navigate the next decade of examination posture comfortably are the ones that approach planning with the documentation, the independence, and the analytical discipline that were always the right approach — and that the Service is now, in effect, requiring.
