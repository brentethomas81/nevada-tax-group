When the Asset Is the Family: Governance Considerations Around a Concentrated Position

The technical estate question is rarely the difficult one. The governance question — who decides what, and when — almost always is.

The first generation builds a single concentrated position. The second generation inherits it. The third generation, in most family histories, no longer has it. The framing is well-worn enough to feel like folklore, but the underlying observation is empirically defensible — and the cause, in most cases the firm has observed, is not tax. It is governance.

Most of the families we work with arrive with the technical estate questions already on the table. Trusts have been drafted. Entities have been organized. A wealth manager has been engaged. The structures are competent, the documentation is current, and the planning, on its own terms, is sound. What is rarely on the table — and what determines, in our experience, whether the planning actually accomplishes what the family intends — is the governance question.

The structures are not the planning

It is possible, and not uncommon, for a family to assemble an estate plan that is technically excellent and operationally fragile. The trust documents are well-drafted. The entity choices are defensible. The transfer techniques have been timed and valued correctly. None of which addresses the underlying question: when the founder is no longer making decisions about this asset, who is, and on what basis?

The answer is rarely as simple as the trust agreement makes it appear. A trustee is named. The trustee has discretion. The discretion is exercised in coordination with — or in tension with — a succession of family members, each with different relationships to the asset, different financial circumstances, and different views about what the prior generation actually wanted.

Most family conflict around inherited wealth is not about tax outcomes. It is about decision rights, communication, and the absence of a forum within which disagreement can be discussed without becoming permanent.

Three governance questions worth answering early

Who decides, and how is that decision made? The trust agreement names a trustee. The operating agreement names a manager. The succession plan names a successor. None of these documents, on their own, describe how a contested decision is actually reached when the founder is no longer in the room. The families who navigate this well have, almost without exception, made some form of governance forum explicit — a family council, an investment committee, an advisory board — and have given that forum a charter.

What does the next generation know, and when do they learn it? The most common failure pattern we see is informational rather than financial. The next generation inherits responsibility for an asset they have not been prepared to operate. The conversations that should have happened across a decade — about how the asset is structured, how it generates returns, what its risks are, how decisions are made about it — happen instead in a single legal meeting following a death.

What is the role of the family, separate from the role of the asset? The most resilient family structures we have observed are those that maintain a clear distinction between the family as a continuing entity and the asset as a financial position. The family persists. The asset may not. Treating those as separate questions, with separate governance, allows the family to make decisions about the asset — including, in some cases, the decision to liquidate it — without those decisions becoming decisions about the family itself.

Governance and structure, properly aligned

The structural choices that surround a concentrated position — directed trust statutes, family limited partnerships, private trust companies, and the trust jurisdictions that support them — are, in our view, properly understood as governance instruments first and tax instruments second. Each of them does, of course, have tax implications. None of them is principally a tax structure.

A directed trust statute is valuable not because it changes a tax outcome but because it allows the family to disaggregate the role of trustee, the role of investment advisor, and the role of distribution committee — assigning each to the party best suited to it, and creating a structure in which those parties must communicate. The tax outcomes are real, but they follow from the governance design, not the other way around.

A family limited partnership is valuable not principally because of the valuation discounts it generates, which the Service has been actively re-examining for two decades, but because it creates a forum in which family members must collectively address questions about a shared asset. The discount, again, is real, but it is a byproduct of an arrangement whose primary work is governance.

The estate planning question that determines whether a family persists across three generations is not which structure is selected. It is which conversations are had.

What this implies about the planning we do

For the families we work with, the governance question is rarely framed in those terms at the outset. The conversation begins with a tax outcome the family is trying to address, an entity the family is trying to organize, or a transaction the family is preparing to undertake. The work, properly done, walks back from those technical questions to the governance questions that surround them.

This is, in some respects, a slower way to practice. It produces shorter engagement letters and longer engagements. It also produces planning that survives the founder, which is, in the end, what almost every family we have worked with says they want.

The technical work matters. It always matters. But the technical work, on its own, is rarely what determines whether the family — and the structures the family has built — are still standing in twenty years.
